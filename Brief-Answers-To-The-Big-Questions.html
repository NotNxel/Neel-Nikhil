Brief Answers To The Big Questions

Stephen Hawking

13-12-2023

< Back

I picked up Brief Answers to the Big Questions expecting a deep dive into Hawkings thoughts on the universe, black holes, and time travel. And while those elements were certainly there, what I didnt expect was the overwhelming sense of humanity that ran through every page. This book isnt just a collection of scientific explanationsits Hawkings final message to the world, a reflection on the biggest existential questions we face as a species. Hawking doesnt shy away from the difficult topics: Is there a God? Will AI replace us? Should we colonize space? How will it all end? These are questions most of us have wondered about at some point, but the way Hawking approaches them is uniquely his own. He breaks down complex scientific ideas into something accessible, but never oversimplifies. Theres a clarity to his writing, a sense that hes speaking not just to scientists or academics, but to anyone who is curious about the fate of humanity. One of the most striking things about the book is its toneboth urgent and optimistic. Hawking doesnt sugarcoat the dangers we face, from climate change to nuclear war to the rise of artificial intelligence. He lays out, in no uncertain terms, that humanity is standing at a crossroads, and the choices we make now will determine whether we thrive or self-destruct. And yet, despite these warnings, there is no cynicism here. Instead, there is an unshakable belief in human ingenuity, in our ability to solve problems, push boundaries, and explore the unknown. His faith in science and progress is evident in every chapter, and by the end, its hard not to feel a little hopeful yourself. Another unexpected aspect of the book is its deeply philosophical nature. While Hawking was first and foremost a physicist, his writing often ventures into questions that are just as much about meaning as they are about mechanics. When he discusses the origins of the universe, he isnt just explaining the Big Banghes addressing the age-old debate about whether the universe needs a creator. When he talks about time travel, it isnt just about equations and paradoxes; its about what it would mean for our understanding of free will and destiny. Science, in his view, is not separate from philosophy but deeply intertwined with it. Perhaps the most profound takeaway from Brief Answers to the Big Questions is the reminder of just how fragile and fleeting human existence is. Compared to the scale of the universe, we are unimaginably small, and our time on Earth is absurdly short. And yet, Hawking argues, that does not make us insignificant. On the contrary, the fact that we can comprehend the very laws that govern realitythat we can ask these big questions and seek answersis something extraordinary. This book didnt necessarily change the way I see the world in a fundamental way, but it did leave me with a renewed sense of wonder. Hawkings words serve as both a warning and an invitation: a warning about the challenges we must overcome and an invitation to keep questioning, keep learning, and keep looking up at the stars. Then you start feeling bad again because everyone else is doing so much, and you're just lagging behind, and you have to be successful too, don't you? So you just pick yourself up and hop on the goddamn fucking hamster wheel all over again until the end of eternity. Or till the end of your life. Before you know it you're hooked onto a ventilator wondering where all the time went, like all the times you've woken up, hopped on Instagram and drugged yourself on 13 hours of meaningless content.


Burkeman made me stop and think the preposterous, horrible thought that maybe the solution isn't doing more things. Absurd, I know. How could we ever succeed in society without being good little slaves that carry progressively more load for arbitrary tasks given to us by people higher up in the social hierarchy and then burn out and feel bad about it? But I do admit that just not doing work was not a satisfying conclusion for me. I wanted to do things, be successful, change the world, yknow? But hey, I'm not done yet - Burkeman's not telling us to simply drop everything and live a life of inaction. The first step is embracing your own limits. Don't think that you're a limitless being - sure, you could do more, but there is a hard cap on how much work you can do. Be cognisant of the fact that you have limits, and work accordingly.

Secondly, work on the right things. A lot of our time is spent on tasks that really serve us no purpose - content addiction is an obvious one. But the problem is a lot more pervasive then it may seem - a lot of us suffer from analysis paralysis, or "productive procrastination". We sharpen our pencils, make that website, come up with schedules and timetables, but we don't really do anything about our goals. Snap out of it. Such actions are only useful if they actually serve as a precursor to true action. There are a lot more incisive tidbits like this scattered throughout the book, along with a few shifts in mental perspective. One of my favourites is our typical view of time itself - more specifically, our perspective of time as a resource. We treat time much like we do money - we spend it, waste it, and quite literallly say "time is money". No it's not.


We don't have a limited amount of time. It might be more appropriate to say that we are a limited amount of time, in the words of Martin Heidegger. There no space out of this quant life that we are afforded that we can take our achievements to - we cannot "get something" out of life, and the very attempt is futile. It is perhaps due to the futility of this action that so much of lives feel hopeless, with seemingly impossible mountains of work to get through. That is just simply not the point of life. Learn to experience, to live, to think for yourself, and to come to terms with your relationship with time. A lot of the book spends time pointing out the obvious flaws in our societal fabric, but the solutions it proposes are weak at best. I didn't really understand what exactly he wanted us to do, which is hypocritical, given my vague statements to "experience" and "enjoy". But most of the value I got from the book wasn't from the proposed solutions - it was in the violent wake-up call that it gave me and the realisation that I was living my life in a manner that was not very satisfying, and that I wanted to change. I'm still learning how to balance lofty goals with quality of life. I have a sometimes counterintuitive, dogged belief that the two are not exclusive of each other, despite all the talk of sacrifice and discipline to the point of self-harm in order to achieve success in mainstream media. Your life can indeed have value without achieving obscene measures of material wealth. It is alright to be tired. It is alright to not always be working.


It is okay to be human.
